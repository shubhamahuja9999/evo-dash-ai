"There’s a lever in Google Ads (Google Partner) most overlook—even advanced advertisers miss this one. It’s called seasonality bid adjustments, and it’s our secret weapon when we need to nudge the system in the right direction.


Here’s how it works: Seasonality adjustments are designed for short-term events—like a 3-day sale—where you expect a conversion spike. Smart Bidding is good, but it’s not psychic. Instead of waiting for Google’s algorithms to catch on, you give it a heads-up: “Expect a 50% bump—adjust accordingly.” After the event? The system resets smoothly.


But here’s where we hack it.


Just like ChatGPT sometimes needs a nudge to give you the right answer, Smart Bidding can get stuck too. For instance, if your campaigns are hitting ROAS targets but Google isn't scaling, it’s likely because the system lacks confidence. That’s when we add a small seasonality adjustment, saying, “Trust us—there’s more room to push.” This trick increases spend without hurting efficiency.


After major events like Black Friday, we do the opposite. Google may overestimate conversion rates, leading to unnecessary spend. A quick adjustment helps us avoid overspending as the hype fades.


Or, during holiday closures, instead of pausing campaigns (and resetting Smart Bidding’s learning phase), we tell Google, “Expect a 90% drop in conversions.” This way, spend drops naturally without breaking performance when things pick back up.


Seasonality bid adjustments let us manipulate Smart Bidding in ways most people don’t even realize.

"Attribution—every advertiser’s Achilles heel. How do we fairly assign credit to each touchpoint in a journey that leads to success?

It sounds simple, but the devil is in the details—especially when you consider attribution windows. This is the timeframe in which interactions are eligible for credit. "Think of your marketing strategy as a high-stakes chess match. Each piece—be it an ad, campaign, or channel—has a strategic role, much like a knight or rook on the board. But how do you truly assess the influence of each move when the game’s success isn’t simply about individual tactics?


Here’s where Brand Lift and the Halo Effect come into play—two crucial concepts that offer a deeper understanding beyond conventional metrics.


Brand Lift is about discerning the broader, often elusive impact of your campaigns. Imagine you hear a podcast ad that compels you to visit a brand’s website and make a purchase. Standard tracking might miss this connection, but advanced analytical models can reveal how this seemingly indirect ad boosted site revenue, even if its influence wasn’t immediately visible.


The Halo Effect, conversely, reveals how one campaign can enhance the performance of another. Consider this: a user encounters a YouTube ad and later searches for your product on Google. "Some ad campaigns are idiotic enough that they warrant a share.


LaGuardia airport, about to board a flight, I need WiFi to send an email I couldn't finish earlier.


But I can't connect ... yet. Because the WiFi is ""Brought to you by HubSpot."" 


Which of course doesn't mean the WiFi is actually brought to me by HubSpot. It just means that HubSpot decided that forcing me to watch a 30-second video about CRM solutions would be a good advertising opportunity.


This is an important distinction. LaGuardia is bringing me the WiFi, right? 


But HubSpot's messaging reinforces that it's HubSpot responsible for, and in control of, the WiFi.


That I'm being maddeningly delayed by a long video is, in the consumers' mind, NOT LaGuardia's fault ... it's HubSpot's.


It's unfair to blame HubSpot.


LaGuardia would have found some other sucker to sponsor their WiFi, and my connection would have been delayed by the same amount of time one way or another.


But what's fair is irrelevant.


Every potential HubSpot prospect blocked from connecting to LaGuardia's free WiFi will come away with a NET NEGATIVE brand affinity toward HubSpot.


Meaning, they'll be LESS likely to ever give their money to HubSpot after seeing their ad than they were before seeing their ad.


This is worse than intrusive advertising.


This is like getting held at gunpoint while the gunman forces you to listen to a sales pitch for a four-way stretch polo shirt he (or she!) designed.


Adding insult to injury is Hubspot's moronic copyrighting AND their manipulative effort to trick you into a click (see the image).


""A CRM so easy to use you can get up and running before you board."" 


HUH? Come again? 


Tell me, seriously, who the hell is signing up for an enterprise Content Management System while they're waiting to board a plane?


No one, that's who.


Then the bright orange ""CONNECT TO WiFi and learn more"" button. It's designed to make you THINK you need to click that orange button—which conveniently directs you to HubSpot's website. 


But of course, you don't need to click ANY buttons. You're already connected to WiFi.


What can we learn from here?


1. Be better than this.


2. Don't look down your nose at your customers. As Ogilvy said, the customer is not dumb. She is your wife.


3. Not all advertising is good advertising. Hijacking the access of a PERCEIVED free utility will piss people off and make them not like you.


4. This isn't rocket science. Just takes a little empathy.


5. If you're going to disrupt someone's day with a message—make it interesting, useful, and relevant. How about this: These five LaGuardia restaurants have the fastest free WiFi, brought to you by HubSpot."

"Are you an #ecommerce marketer? Want to improve your conversion rate?


Tip: Product descriptions should help website visitors identify THEMSELVES.


Take a queue from Garmin, who nails self-identification on this category page. 


Web designers/marketers tend to over-describe their products but fail to help users identify which product is right for THEM.


Look at these e-commerce product descriptions for a range of Garmin bike computers. Lots of computers to choose from, and it's not obvious which computer is right for you.


""You just want to enjoy the ride with a simple computer ...""
""You're an eBike rider ...""
""You're a performance rider ...""


These descriptions prioritize identifying the customer before identifying the product features.


Go look at your product descriptions, especially on your category overview pages. Are you identifying the customer or only extolling the product's features?


One of the most powerful #cro tips that I talk about often is using product descriptions to help your website visitors identify themselves first ... and then highlight the key features once that relevancy has been established.
